The Science Behind The Match

Why finding a donor is like searching for a genetic twin

It's Not About Blood Type

While blood type matters for transfusions, stem cell donation requires matching Human Leukocyte Antigens (HLA) - proteins found on most cells in your body. These act as your immune system's identification markers.

HLA markers are inherited from both parents. You have a 25% chance of matching a sibling, but only a 1 in 1 million chance with a stranger.

The Diversity Problem

HLA markers are ethnically linked. Indians have unique genetic profiles, but our representation in global registries is critically low. Only 0.04% of India's population is registered as potential donors.

30% of patients find no match

How HLA Matching Works

Your immune system uses HLA markers to distinguish "self" from "foreign." A transplant requires at least a 10/10 match across key HLA genes (A, B, C, DR, DQ). Even a 9/10 match can cause graft rejection or Graft-versus-Host Disease (GvHD).

Movie Myths vs. Medical Reality

Let's separate Hollywood fiction from actual science

The Myth

"It's a painful spinal surgery"

Movies show donors being put under anesthesia with giant needles extracting bone marrow from the spine. Dramatic? Yes. Accurate? Not anymore.

"Recovery takes months"

The old bone marrow harvesting method required hospital stays and weeks of recovery. Modern methods have changed everything.

"Your own health is at risk"

Stem cells regenerate within 4-6 weeks. You won't be "weaker" or lose immunity. Your body produces millions of stem cells daily.

The Reality

PBSC Method: Like Blood Donation

90% of donations now use Peripheral Blood Stem Cell (PBSC) collection. You sit in a chair for 4-6 hours while a machine collects stem cells from your bloodstream via an IV - similar to donating platelets.

Pre-donation: You'll receive injections of Filgrastim for 4-5 days to boost stem cell production. Side effects: bone aches (like flu symptoms) that resolve within 2 days post-donation.

Back to Normal in Days

Most donors return to work within 1-2 days. No surgery scars. No long-term effects. Just temporary fatigue and achiness.

Full Medical Coverage

DKMS covers all costs: medical exams, travel, accommodation, lost wages. You pay nothing. Your insurance isn't touched.

What About the Other 10%?

In rare cases, doctors may request bone marrow harvesting (the old method) if the patient needs specific cell types. This involves general anesthesia and extracting marrow from the pelvic bone. Recovery is 2-4 weeks with soreness at the extraction site. You can decline this method if you're only comfortable with PBSC.

You'll always be informed of the method before confirming donation

Can You Register?

Quick eligibility check - these are safety guidelines, not personal judgments

You're Good to Go If:

  • Age: 18-55 years

    You can register until age 55, but you're eligible to donate until age 61

  • BMI: 18-40

    Higher BMI affects anesthesia safety if bone marrow harvesting is needed

  • Generally Healthy

    No chronic conditions that affect blood or immunity

  • Pregnancy/Breastfeeding

    You can register, but actual donation must wait until 6 months post-delivery/weaning

You Cannot Register If:

  • Blood-Borne Infections

    HIV, Hepatitis B/C, HTLV, syphilis

  • Autoimmune Diseases

    Lupus, rheumatoid arthritis, Crohn's, Type 1 diabetes

  • Chronic Lung/Heart Issues

    Severe asthma, COPD, heart disease

  • Active Cancer History

    Current cancer or treatment within the past 5 years

  • IV Drug Use

    Risk of undetected blood-borne pathogens

If you're on the fence about a specific condition, register anyway! DKMS will conduct a full health screening if you're ever matched. It's better to be on the registry and potentially excluded later than to never register at all.

Medical guidelines are reviewed case-by-case. Past depression, mild asthma, or controlled hypertension don't automatically disqualify you.
